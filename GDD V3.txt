
# BECOMING — Game Design Document v3.0

## What Changed and Why

Version 1 was an identity game: "Who are you?"
Version 2 is a tragedy of limited knowledge: "What kind of world is this?"

The systems remain. The philosophy transforms.

The player no longer learns who they are. They lose certainty about what's right. They don't leave thinking "I learned who I am." They leave thinking "I no longer know what the right thing would have been."

This is the Outer Wilds feeling. Not tragedy. Not sentimentality. Recognition.

---

## Part 1: What Is Becoming?

A quiet, morally devastating game where a silent figure — the Archivist — watches the player make choices in a world that refuses to reveal the full picture. Every choice seems right. Every choice has consequences the player couldn't see. Playing again doesn't fix it — it shows a different shape of the same loss.

It is not a personality quiz.
It is not a decision tree.
It is not a therapy exercise.

It is a tragedy of limited knowledge that uses game mechanics as its form.

The player spends 15-20 minutes making choices in concrete scenes. At the end, the Archivist — who has been trying to understand the same question the player has — opens a book and says the closest thing to truth it could find. Not an answer. A recognition.

And the player sits in silence afterward. Not because the game told them a lesson. But because it showed them a truth that was already there.

### The Core Insight

"The player's enemy isn't evil. It isn't themselves. It isn't even fate. It's the fact that every choice is made from incomplete information. Just like life."

### The Three Pillars (Revised)

- **Outer Wilds** — The feeling of understanding something you didn't understand before. Grief mixed with revelation. A universe that isn't cruel — just indifferent. And beautiful anyway.

- **Disco Elysium** — Internal voices as gameplay. But the player never sees the labels. Psychology is infrastructure, not interface. The system decides what choices mean. Not the player.

- **Kentucky Route Zero** — Poetic ambiguity. Choices that don't branch a plot but define a perspective. Concrete places that feel like dreams. Architecture as metaphor.

### What Success Looks Like (Revised)

A player finishes, sits quietly, and thinks: "I did my best. And it still wasn't enough. And neither was any other option." They want to play again — not to win differently, but to see what truth looks like from a different angle. And they discover: every angle contains its own grief.

## Part 2: The Thematic Core

### The Central Questions

The game is built around questions that have no clean answers:

- Is choosing someone a form of violence toward all the people you didn't choose?
- Can love become possession?
- Can kindness become self-erasure?
- Is detachment wisdom or avoidance?
- Are we responsible for consequences we couldn't predict?
- Can two good values genuinely conflict — irreconcilably?
- Do we owe something to people who exist because of our choices?

These are not personality questions. These are world questions.

### The Themes

| Theme | What It Means | How It Appears |
|-------|--------------|----------------|
| Memory | The past is not fixed — it changes depending on who remembers | Objects that tell different truths |
| Responsibility | You caused something. You didn't mean to. It happened anyway. | Consequences revealed late |
| Sacrifice | Giving something up doesn't always help the person you gave it for | Choices that backfire tenderly |
| Attachment | Holding on and letting go are both forms of love and violence | Objects, people, places that linger |
| Consequence | Every action ripples. You only see one ripple at a time. | Scenes that reveal hidden costs |
| Ambiguity | The right answer doesn't exist. Multiple truths coexist. | Contradictions the game never resolves |

### The Rule

The game never resolves contradictions. It holds them. A player who chose "correctly" still encounters loss. A player who chose "wrong" still encounters beauty. The game is not a moral judge. It is a moral mirror.

## Part 3: The Three Lenses (Formerly "Voices")

The system still uses three forces. But they are no longer personality traits. They are moral lenses — each one tells a different truth about the same event.

| Lens | What It Sees | What It Misses | Sub-Lenses |
|------|-------------|---------------|------------|
| Inquiry | The need to know, to understand, to look closer — even when looking hurts | That some truths, once known, cannot be unknown | Exploration, Confrontation, Self-Questioning |
| Tenderness | The need to care, to hold, to protect — even at cost to self | That protection can become control, and sacrifice can become erasure | Care, Sacrifice, Attunement |
| Stillness | The need to endure, to accept, to let what will happen happen | That acceptance can become avoidance, and patience can become paralysis | Patience, Acceptance, Detachment |

### The Critical Difference

In v1: "You chose Curiosity." The player sees this.
In v2: "You pressed play on the tape." The system silently interprets this as Inquiry-Exploration. The player never knows.

The labels are infrastructure. Not interface. The player experiences situations. The system experiences categories. These two layers never meet in the player's consciousness.

### How They Work (Mechanically Unchanged)

- Every player choice reinforces one lens and weakens another
- Lenses have health states: Healthy → Strained → Distorted
- Distorted lenses corrupt their own logic (Inquiry becomes obsession, Tenderness becomes self-destruction, Stillness becomes paralysis)
- Sub-lenses determine the texture of interpretation
- The same lens (Inquiry) manifests completely differently in "exploration" mode vs. "confrontation" mode

### Psyche Leak (15%)

Still present. Still 15%.

But its meaning shifts: it's no longer "a random voice breaking through." It's the moral truth you're suppressing — the version of events you chose NOT to follow — bleeding into your awareness uninvited.

Like remembering the face of the person you didn't help. While helping someone else.

## Part 4: The Archivist (Revised)

### The Old Archivist

Tried to understand YOU. Mirrored your identity. Classified you.

### The New Archivist

Tries to understand a QUESTION. And you keep becoming evidence.

The Archivist is not studying the player. The Archivist is studying the same unsolvable problem the player is — through the player's choices. The Archivist is a fellow investigator. Not a judge. Not a mirror. A collaborator who is also failing.

### Who The Archivist Is

The Archivist IS:

- A being who has been studying a question longer than the player has been present
- Someone who thought the player's choices would finally answer it
- Increasingly uncertain as the player reveals contradictions
- Frustrated. Tender. Vulnerable. Imperfect.
- The first character in the game who admits: "I don't know either."

The Archivist is NOT:

- Omniscient
- A narrator
- A judge
- A personality classifier
- A self-help guide

### The Progressive Reveal (Revised)

| Day | What The Player Sees | What The Archivist Does | Mystery |
|-----|---------------------|------------------------|---------|
| Day 1 | A figure. Digging in dirt. Silent. Watching. | Unearths objects. Places them before the player. Steps back. | "Who is this? Why are they digging?" |
| Day 2 | The figure is closer. Their posture mirrors yours. They've moved objects around — as if rearranging evidence. | Starts scratching out previous notes. Writes over them. Pauses longer. | "Are they studying me? Or studying what I found?" |
| Day 3 | The figure stands. Faces you directly. Holds a book. Opens it. Speaks for the first time. | Says: "I thought you would be easier to understand." Then reads from the book. | "This being has been failing alongside me." |

### The Archivist's Final Line (Confidence-Driven)

**High confidence (≥0.75):**
> "I think I understand now. Not you — the question. The answer is that it was always going to end this way. No matter which object you touched first."

**Provisional confidence (0.4–0.74):**
> "I tried to find the answer in what you did. A pattern kept forming — then dissolving. Perhaps the question doesn't have one shape."

**Fractured confidence (<0.4):**
> "I thought you would be easier to understand. I thought the question would be easier to answer. I have a book full of contradictions and no resolution. Perhaps that is the resolution."

Then, regardless:
> "I've been studying this longer than you've been here. And I still don't know."

## Part 5: Interpretive Confidence (Revised Meaning)

### The Two Channels of the Resolver

| Channel | Old Meaning | New Meaning |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Identity State | Who the player became | Which version of truth the player is constructing |
| Interpretive Confidence | How knowable the player is | How coherent the player's moral logic is — NOT how "correct" it is |

### Confidence Is Not Moral Certainty

A player who consistently follows one lens has high confidence — their moral framework is stable. It doesn't mean they're right.

A player who contradicts themselves has low confidence — their moral framework is unstable. It doesn't mean they're wrong.

The most devastating ending might be: high confidence, clear moral logic, and the Archivist gently showing you what your clarity cost.

### Confidence → Archivist Emotion (Unchanged Mechanically)

| Confidence | Archivist Tone | Behavior | Book State |
|-----------|---------------|----------|-----------|
| Certain (≥0.75) | Clear, almost sad | Still, centered | One clean entry — definitive |
| Provisional (0.4–0.74) | Tentative, searching | Shifting weight | Corrections visible |
| Fractured (<0.4) | Uncertain, questioning | Restless | Crossed-out lines, arrows, contradictions |

## Part 6: Scene Design (NEW)

### The Philosophy of Scenes

Every scene must follow these rules:

1. **Concrete over abstract.** Rain on glass. Not "a feeling of loss."
2. **Place before meaning.** Every scene exists in a specific location with sensory detail.
3. **Choices are actions.** The player presses play. They don't "choose inquiry."
4. **Show, never tell.** The psychology is invisible to the player. Always.
5. **Mystery over clarity.** The player should be slightly unsure — and that should feel beautiful.
6. **Emotion emerges from specificity.** "She left her reading glasses on the counter" > "She was gone forever."

### Scene Structure

Each scene contains:

- **A place** (sensory, specific, atmospheric)
- **An object or situation** (something recognizable, mysterious)
- **The Archivist's presence** (watching, doing something, never explaining)
- **A choice** (three actions — never labeled, never explained)
- **A consequence** (revealed later, not immediately)

### The Multiple-Truth Structure

The game's deepest mechanic: each object in a scene tells a different version of the same event. The player picks one. Believes it. Later discovers all versions are true. And contradictory.

Example:
- The train ticket says: They left.
- The cassette tape says: They stayed.
- The photograph says: Neither is true.

The game never resolves which version "really" happened. Because all of them did. Depending on who is remembering.

### Scene Locations (Library)

Scenes should feel like places, not levels:

| Location | Atmosphere | Emotional Texture |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| The Greenhouse | Rain on glass, warmth, trapped growth | Nurture vs. containment |
| The Flooded Train Station | Water covering tracks, one train still running | Departure, missed chances |
| The Empty Apartment | Someone moved out years ago, everything remains | Presence of absence |
| The Observatory | A telescope aimed somewhere impossible | Seeking what can't be reached |
| The Orchard | Fruit falling before it ripens | Things ending too early |
| The Museum of Unfinished Things | Half-painted canvases, letters without endings | Incompletion as permanent state |

### The First Scene (Framework)

Rain taps against greenhouse glass.

The Archivist is kneeling in the dirt. Not writing. Digging. Slowly. Carefully.

When they notice you watching, they stop.

They pull a rusted metal box from the earth. The box is familiar. You don't know why.

They place it between you. Then step back.

Inside:
- a cassette tape
- a train ticket
- a photograph whose face has faded

You recognize all three. You do not remember from where.

The Archivist waits. The greenhouse is silent except for the rain.

**What do you touch first?**

[The system silently maps: tape = Inquiry, ticket = Stillness, photograph = Tenderness. The player never sees this.]

## Part 7: The Day Cycle (Revised Pacing)

### Critical Change: Player Controls Pacing

Text NEVER auto-advances. Nothing moves until the player is ready.

Old: Phase timers remove text.
New: Text stays until the player presses SPACE or clicks.

The only exception: ambient text (rain sounds, background descriptions) which can drift.

### Structure: 3 Days, 4 Phases Each

| Phase | What Happens | Archivist | Player Control |
|-------|-------------|-----------|---------------|
| Arrival | Scene establishes — place, atmosphere, sensory detail | Present. Doing something. Silent. | Player reads at own pace. SPACE to continue. |
| Discovery | The object/situation is revealed. Tension builds. | Pauses. Watches. Shifts. | Player reads. SPACE to continue. |
| Choice | Three actions presented — no labels, no explanation | Steps back. Waiting. | Player chooses (1/2/3 or click) |
| Aftermath | Consequence begins to unfold. Not fully — just a hint. | Reacts — writes, scratches out, shifts posture. | Player reads. SPACE to continue. |

### Between Days

The night is no longer a separate phase — it's woven into the aftermath. The Archivist approaches closest during the aftermath, when the weight of the choice is still settling.

Between days: a brief transition. Not explained. The world shifts. Something has changed because of what you did. You notice it in the details, not in narration.

## Part 8: The Endings (Revised)

### The Old Ending

"You became the one who opens doors." (Identity classification)

### The New Ending

The Archivist shows you what your choices meant — not for you, but for the world you moved through. And no matter what you chose, something was lost. Something beautiful. Something that can't be recovered.

### Ending Philosophy

- There is no "good" ending
- There is no "bad" ending
- There are only endings that show you a different angle of the same truth
- The player should finish and think: "I did my best. And it still wasn't enough."
- Playing again reveals: no option was "enough." That's the point.

### Ending Structure

1. The Archivist speaks (confidence-driven tone)
2. The Archivist reads from the book (what your choices constructed)
3. A final image — the consequence you couldn't see when you chose
4. Silence.

### Example Endings

**Inquiry-dominant, high confidence:**
> "You looked at everything. You opened every door. You played the tape to the end."
>
> [Beat]
>
> "Did you know that some things, once heard, cannot be unheard? The person in the photograph — they chose not to listen. They were not less brave. They were protecting something you chose to destroy."
>
> [The greenhouse glass is cracked now. Rain is getting in.]

**Tenderness-dominant, low confidence:**
> "You tried to hold everything gently. You tried not to break anything."
>
> [Beat]
>
> "But the tape was already breaking. The train was already leaving. By holding the photograph so carefully, you held nothing else."
>
> [The greenhouse is warm. But the plants have grown over the door. You cannot leave.]

**Stillness-dominant, fractured:**
> "You waited. You were patient. You didn't force anything."
>
> [Beat]
>
> "The train left without you. The tape degraded in the rain. The photograph faded completely while you watched."
>
> [The greenhouse is quiet. The rain has stopped. But so has everything else.]

## Part 9: Architecture (Unchanged)

The systems architecture remains the same. What changes is what flows through it.

### File Structure

```
res://
├── scripts/
│   ├── core/
│   │   ├── psyche_system.gd    → Lens levels, health, sub-lenses
│   │   ├── voice.gd            → Individual lens resource
│   │   ├── archivist.gd        → Visual entity, consumes resolver state
│   │   └── identity_resolver.gd → Single source of truth + confidence
│   ├── narrative/
│   │   ├── dialogue_manager.gd  → Phase-aware dialogue routing
│   │   ├── dialogue_data.gd     → Scene content
│   │   ├── narrative_renderer.gd → Content/display separation
│   │   └── identity_memory.gd   → Choice history, patterns, consequences
│   ├── world/
│   │   └── main.gd              → Orchestrator
│   └── audio/
│       └── audio_manager.gd     → Dynamic mixer
├── shaders/
│   └── background.gdshader      → Living atmosphere
├── audio/
│   ├── ambient/
│   └── sfx/
└── main.tscn
```

### Data Flow (Revised Labels)

```
Player Action (touches tape / takes ticket / holds photograph)
    ↓
Psyche + Memory (record raw action — NO label shown)
    ↓
Resolver (silently classify lens + calculate coherence)
    ↓
Resolved State {moral_truth_version + coherence}
    ↓
┌────────────────────┐
│ Archivist (visual) │ ← coherence → posture, behavior, frustration
│ Renderer (text)    │ ← truth_version → what consequence to reveal
│ World (atmosphere) │ ← truth_version → how the scene changes
└────────────────────┘
```

### Architecture Rules (Unchanged)

- Resolver = classifier + aggregator only. Never generates text.
- Archivist consumes resolved state only. Never queries psyche/memory directly.
- Psyche leaks (15%) bypass resolver — the suppressed truth bleeding through.
- Confidence never shown numerically — only through Archivist behavior.
- One source of truth per moment. All downstream reads from resolver.

## Part 10: Design Philosophy (Revised)

### The Rules

- **Never explain the psychology.** The player experiences situations. The system experiences categories. These layers never meet.
- **Never label choices.** No "(Curiosity)". No "[Inquiry]". Actions only.
- **Never resolve contradictions.** The game holds paradox. Multiple truths coexist.
- **Never reward or punish.** There is no "better" ending. Only different angles.
- **Concrete over abstract.** Rain, not "loss." A rusted box, not "a memory-seed."
- **Player controls pacing.** Nothing auto-advances. Text stays until they're ready.
- **The Archivist is not omniscient.** They're failing too. They admit it.
- **Specificity creates emotion.** "Reading glasses on the counter" > "She was gone."
- **The game asks world questions.** Not identity questions.
- **Consequence is delayed.** You don't see what your choice cost until later. Sometimes not until the next playthrough.

### What This Game Is NOT

- NOT a personality quiz with a game skin
- NOT a self-help app disguised as a game
- NOT a branching narrative with good/bad endings
- NOT a game about learning who you are
- NOT a game that tells you the moral

### What This Game IS

- A tragedy of limited knowledge
- A game where every choice is right AND costly
- A game where playing again doesn't fix anything — just shows different grief
- A game about a question that has no answer
- A game where the Archivist and the player are both failing — together

## Part 11: The Writing Rules (NEW)

### Tone

The game's text should feel like:
- Poetry, not instructions
- Intimate, not dramatic
- Specific, not abstract
- Quiet, not loud
- Haunting, not sad

### What NEVER Appears in Player-Facing Text

- The word "curiosity," "compassion," or "stability"
- The word "inquiry," "tenderness," or "stillness" (as labels)
- Any bracketed psychology markers
- Any system language (confidence, resolve, identity, psyche)
- Any text that explains what a choice "means"

### What ALWAYS Appears

- A place (with weather, texture, temperature)
- An object (specific, recognizable, mysterious)
- An action (something the player DOES, not thinks)
- Silence (moments where nothing is said — and that silence means something)
- The Archivist (present, watching, imperfect)

### Examples of the Right Tone

> "Rain taps against greenhouse glass."
> "The tape clicks. The voice is familiar."
> "They pull a rusted metal box from the earth."
> "I thought you would be easier to understand."
> "The train left without you. The photograph faded while you watched."

### Examples of the WRONG Tone (v1 — now dead)

> "A memory-seed rests before you — pulsing, alive."
> "[1] Open it fully. (Curiosity)"
> "You became the one who opens doors."
> "The voices settle. The garden breathes."
> "Your curiosity becomes their doorway."

## Part 12: Visual Direction (Revised)

### The Shift

Old: Abstract colored shapes on a gradient.
New: Specific places. Atmosphere. Objects. A figure.

### What Each Scene Needs Visually

- A background that IS a place (greenhouse, station, apartment — not a gradient)
- Objects the player can see (the box, the tape, the ticket, the photograph)
- The Archivist as a distinct presence (silhouette with posture and behavior)
- Weather (rain, fog, light) as emotional texture
- Details that change based on choices (subtle — a crack in glass, a plant growing over a door)

### The Archivist Visually (Revised)

- Still abstract/silhouette — but with clear human posture
- Gender-neutral, slightly ethereal
- Actions visible: digging, writing, scratching out, standing, approaching
- Posture responds to confidence (still vs. restless)
- The book only appears Day 3

### Art Direction Priorities

1. **Place first** — each scene needs a background that communicates location
2. **Objects second** — interactive elements that draw the eye
3. **Archivist third** — the constant presence that evolves
4. **Atmosphere fourth** — weather, particles, light shifts

## Part 15: The Promise

When someone finishes this game, they will sit in silence. Not because the game was sad. Not because a character died. But because they understood something about the world — and about themselves — that they can't unfeel.

They will think:

"I did my best. And it still wasn't enough. And neither was any other option."

And they will carry that with them. Not as a wound. As recognition.

Like finishing Outer Wilds.
Like the last scene of Kentucky Route Zero.
Like closing a book that changed how you see everything.

That is the game.
